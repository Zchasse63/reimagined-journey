Aluminum foil pans are the workhorse of catering, steam-table service, oven baking, and high-volume takeout. Two specs determine almost everything: **size** (full, half, quarter, oblong, round) and **gauge** (wall thickness, denominated in grams per square inch). Pair each pan with the matching lid and you have an operation. This guide covers the sizing system, gauge-to-application matchup, lid pairings, and the tariff backdrop that's reshaped sourcing economics since 2023.

## The US foodservice sizing system

Aluminum catering pans use a standardized sizing system that maps directly to steam-table well dimensions. Memorize five sizes and you can spec any catering setup:

| Size | Dimensions (W × D × H) | Typical capacity | Feeds (entrée + 2 sides) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Full size** | 20-13/16" × 12-13/16" × 2-9/16" | 7–8 quarts | 24–30 people |
| **Full size deep** | 20-13/16" × 12-13/16" × 4" | 12 quarts | 35–45 people |
| **Half size** | 12-3/4" × 10-3/8" × 2-1/2" | 3 quarts | 12–15 people |
| **Quarter size** | 6-1/2" × 10-3/8" × 2-1/2" | 1.5 quarts | 6–8 people |
| **7" round** | 7" diameter × 2" | 1 quart | 1–2 people |
| **9" round** | 9" diameter × 2" | 2 quarts | 3–4 people |
| **Oblong (2 lb / 5 lb / 8 lb / 14 lb)** | various | various | Family takeout |

A standard 6-foot rectangular catering buffet table holds 3 full-size pans side-by-side or 6 half-size pans. Build your menu around full or half sizes for the bulk of buffet service; reach for quarter sizes for high-cost garnishes (where waste matters) or low-demand items.

## Gauge selection by application

Pan gauge is the spec that buyers most often get wrong. Light-gauge pans dent during transit and warp under hot food weight; over-spec'd pans cost more than necessary. Quick reference:

| Gauge | Use case | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **7g–9g (light)** | Cold catering, single-meal takeout, prep | Easily dents — risky for transit-heavy ops |
| **13g–14g (medium-heavy)** | Hot catering, steam table, oven hold | The industry workhorse — most catering pans are this weight |
| **33g+ (extra heavy)** | Oven baking, dense food, premium catering presentation | Highest cost; structurally rigid even when full |

For most catering operations, **13g is the right default for full size, half size, and quarter size pans**. It survives transit, holds shape when full of hot food, and is oven-safe to 500°F. Step up to 33g+ only when you're presenting at the table (the gauge is visible in how the pan holds shape) or doing real high-heat baking.

## Pan-and-lid pairings

Three lid types cover virtually all catering use cases. Pair the lid to what the pan does next after it leaves the kitchen:

<CatalogLink slug="aluminum_container_lids" cta="Browse aluminum container lids →" />

**Foil-laminated board lids** — the standard transport lid. Lightweight foil bonded to paperboard. Sealable, low cost, ideal for delivery from kitchen to event venue. NOT oven-safe (paperboard scorches around 350°F). Match the board lid weight to pan weight — heavy pan + light board lid is a recipe for warping during steam-table holding.

**Solid aluminum lids** — heavy oven and steam-table use. The pan and lid travel together through the oven, hold on the steam table, and serve from the same vessel. Higher cost but the right call for hot-buffet catering where the same pan does multi-hour holding.

**Plastic dome lids** (PET or PP) — cold display and self-service catering setups where customers see the food without opening. PET dome lids pair with cold service; PP dome lids are microwave-safe.

## Oven-to-steam-table workflows

The catering workflow most aluminum pans serve looks like this:

1. **Prep** (commissary kitchen): Food goes into the pan, gets covered with foil or solid lid.
2. **Reheat / hold** (oven at 250–350°F): Pan + lid system goes into the oven for 30–60 minutes to reach serving temp.
3. **Transit** (insulated bag or hot box): Pan + lid system, sealed.
4. **Steam-table hold** (140°F+ for FDA food safety compliance): Pan goes directly into the steam well, lid stays on between service rounds.

For this workflow, 13g pans with **either board lids (if oven temp ≤350°F) or solid aluminum lids (if higher)** are the right pairing. Don't use plastic dome lids in this workflow — they'll soften in the oven and crack when transferred to the steam table.

## Tariff impact on aluminum pricing (current as of 2026)

The price you pay for aluminum pans in 2026 reflects a stack of trade duties that wasn't present in 2022:

- **Section 301 tariff** (China-origin only): 25% on top of the base rate
- **Antidumping / Countervailing duties (AD/CVD)** on Chinese aluminum foil: 48.64% – 106.09%
- **Base HTS duty** on aluminum foil pans (HTS 7612.90.10): 5.4%

Combined, the effective landed cost increase on China-origin aluminum foil pans has exceeded **130%** since 2024. US distributors responded by shifting sourcing to Turkey, Vietnam, Korea, and domestic mills. Some pre-tariff inventory still trickles through at lower prices, but new orders all reflect the current landed reality. If your aluminum pan pricing seems high in 2026 vs your 2022 contract — that's why.

## Aluminum foil rolls and pop-up sheets

A catering operation runs through more foil rolls than people realize. The 18" × 500' standard roll is the catering workhorse; pop-up interfolded sheets (9"×10.75" or 12"×10.75") earn their cost premium in high-volume wrap stations. Heavy-duty foil (~1.0 mil thickness) holds up to the oven; standard foil (~0.6 mil) is wrap-and-transit grade.
