Compostable foodservice packaging is no longer a niche category — it's mandated in growing parts of the country and central to brand positioning in eco-aware markets. The category is also one of the most misunderstood, because "compostable" means very different things depending on whether industrial composting infrastructure exists in the buyer's market. This guide walks through the five materials in current circulation, what BPI certification actually guarantees, where it's mandated, and how to source intelligently in 2026.

## The five compostable materials in foodservice

| Material | What it is | Best for | Heat tolerance | Cost vs. petroleum |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Bagasse** (sugarcane pulp fiber) | Fibrous byproduct of sugarcane sugar production, molded into rigid shapes | Plates, bowls, clamshells | ~200°F | +30–60% vs. PET |
| **Kraft paper / unbleached fiber** | Natural pulp paper, often recycled-content | Bags, wraps, plates | High (paper-grade) | Parity to small premium |
| **PLA** (polylactic acid) | Bioplastic from corn or sugarcane starch | Cold cups, cutlery, clear films | ~113°F (warps above) | +50–100% vs. PET |
| **CPLA** (crystallized PLA) | Heat-treated PLA, more rigid and heat-tolerant | Hot cutlery, lids, hot beverage applications | ~200°F | +150–250% vs. PP |
| **PHA** (polyhydroxyalkanoate) | Newer bioplastic, fermented from plant sugars | Specialty films, premium applications | ~140°F | High (still scaling production) |

For most foodservice operations, the working combinations are:

- **Bagasse clamshells + kraft bags + CPLA cutlery** — full meal kit, hot-food capable
- **Bagasse plates + PLA cold cups + kraft napkins** — fast-casual / coffee shop
- **Kraft bags + bagasse plates + wooden cutlery** — simplest entry-level kit

## What "BPI certified" guarantees and what it doesn't

The BPI (Biodegradable Products Institute) certification is the US foodservice standard for compostability. The certification requires:

- **Disintegration**: 90% breakdown within 84 days at industrial composting temperatures (~140°F)
- **Biodegradation**: 90% conversion to CO₂ within 180 days
- **No toxic residue**: heavy metals and PFAS limits

What BPI certification does NOT guarantee:

- **Home composting**. BPI certified ≠ home compostable. Home compost bins typically don't reach 140°F+. A bagasse plate in a backyard compost pile may sit there for years.
- **Landfill biodegradation**. Landfills are anaerobic (no oxygen) — compostables don't break down meaningfully there. Methane generation increases, which is environmentally worse than inert plastic.
- **Curbside organics acceptance**. Whether your local hauler will pick up BPI-certified items depends on the local facility, not the product. Always confirm acceptance with your local organics provider.

The TL;DR: **BPI-certified compostable products work as a system, not as products in isolation.** They're the right choice when industrial composting infrastructure exists or is mandated. They're an environmental wash (or net negative due to higher manufacturing energy) when they go to landfill.

## Where compostable packaging is mandated

The mandate landscape has shifted rapidly. As of 2026, the major mandates affecting foodservice packaging are:

**State-level:**
- **Washington (SB 5022, organics diversion 2030)** — food businesses generating 8+ tons/year of organics must source-separate; foodservice packaging composability matters for the organics stream.
- **California (SB 1383, AB 1276)** — businesses must offer compostable or reusable cutlery on request only (cutlery-on-request), and large generators must divert organics.
- **New York (statewide foam ban + NYC plastic cutlery on-request)** — drives demand for compostable alternatives even where not explicitly mandated.
- **Maine, Maryland, Vermont, Oregon (foam bans)** — push the cold-food market toward fiber clamshells and PLA-lined alternatives.

**Federal:** No federal mandate yet, but FDA is finalizing PFAS bans on foodservice packaging which affects compostables coated with PFAS for grease resistance.

For up-to-date state mandates, see our companion guide on [state-by-state foodservice plastic bans](/guides/plastic-bans-by-state-2026/) and [PFAS bans](/guides/pfas-bans-foodservice-packaging/).

## PFAS — the hidden compostable issue

Until 2022, most "compostable" fiber clamshells used PFAS chemicals (per- and poly-fluoroalkyl substances) to add grease resistance. PFAS is now widely regulated as a "forever chemical" — it doesn't break down in the environment and accumulates in living organisms.

Multiple states (CA, NY, ME, MN, WA) have banned PFAS in foodservice packaging including in BPI-certified compostables. The compostable industry has shifted to PFAS-free alternatives but you must explicitly ask for PFAS-free fiber products. A 2024 audit found 30%+ of "compostable" containers on the US market still contained PFAS at non-trace levels.

**When sourcing fiber clamshells and bags, look for:**
- Explicit "PFAS-free" or "no added fluorinated chemicals" claims
- BPI certification (which now requires PFAS-free)
- Third-party PFAS testing certificates from the vendor

## Sourcing compostable bags

Kraft paper bags (SOS, twisted-handle, liquor) are inherently compostable when uncoated and free of synthetic adhesives. They're the easiest compostable product to source confidently — no PFAS concerns, no industrial-composting-only caveats, no heat-tolerance issues.

The premium options are:
- **Recycled-content kraft** — 30–100% post-consumer recycled fiber
- **FSC-certified kraft** — sustainably-managed forest sourcing
- **Bleached white kraft** — for premium brand positioning (compostable but slightly less eco-positioned than natural kraft)

| PET clamshell vs bagasse clamshell | $0.18 each | $0.28 each | +56% |
| PET cold cup vs PLA cold cup | $0.08 each | $0.14 each | +75% |
| PP heavy-weight cutlery vs CPLA cutlery | $0.012 each | $0.032 each | +167% |
| Kraft SOS bag (compostable) vs same | (already compostable) | — | — |
| Plain PE-coated paper hot cup vs uncoated/PLA-lined | $0.05 each | $0.09 each | +80% |

Most operations absorb the premium when:
1. Operating in a mandate market (no choice)
2. Eco-positioning is part of the brand value (the premium funds the brand signal)
3. Customers actively prefer it (some demographics genuinely pay attention)

Operations that try to absorb compostable pricing without #1, #2, or #3 typically revert within 6–12 months.
