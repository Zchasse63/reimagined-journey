A clamshell is the workhorse container of foodservice takeout. The category name describes a shape (hinged, two halves that snap shut) but covers half a dozen materials — each with different temperature ranges, recyclability, and price points. Picking the right material matters more than picking the right brand. This guide walks through the five clamshell material types in current circulation, when to use each, and the tradeoffs that show up at scale.

## The five clamshell materials

| Material | Resin code | Cold | Hot | Recyclable | Compostable | Typical cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **PET** (polyethylene terephthalate) | #1 | ✅ | ❌ | ✅ | ❌ | $$ |
| **PP** (polypropylene) | #5 | ✅ | ✅ (to ~230°F) | Theoretically | ❌ | $$ |
| **MFPP** (microwavable PP) | #5 | ✅ | ✅ (microwave-safe) | Theoretically | ❌ | $$$ |
| **PS / Foam EPS** | #6 | ✅ | ✅ (insulating) | ❌ | ❌ | $ (banned in 10+ states) |
| **Fiber** (bagasse / kraft) | n/a | ✅ | ✅ (uncoated to ~200°F) | ❌ | ✅ (BPI) | $$$ |

A working assumption that holds 90% of the time: **use PET for cold food, MFPP for hot, fiber for compostable mandate markets.** Foam is on the decline. Plain PP without microwave-rating is a cost compromise that's hard to justify when MFPP exists.

## PET — the cold-food default

PET clamshells are the right call for any cold foodservice application where visibility matters. Salads, sandwiches, bakery items, fresh-cut produce, deli prepared meals, sushi. The optical clarity is the selling feature — customers see the product through the package without opening it, which drives impulse purchases in retail display cases and reduces "is this what I ordered" anxiety in takeout.

What PET clamshells **cannot** do:
- Microwave reheat. PET warps at 160°F. Use MFPP for microwavable hot food.
- Oven service. PET will melt entirely above 200°F.
- Long-term hot holding. Hot soup in a PET clamshell will warp the lid within 20 minutes.

PET is recyclable (resin code #1) and accepted in most US municipal curbside programs that take rigid plastic. Acceptance varies — bagged or contaminated PET is rejected. The crystal-clear single-material construction also makes PET the easiest plastic to recycle when consumers actually do it.

### Common PET sizes

Standard footprints are 6×6×3, 8×8×3, 9×6×3, 9×9×3 (inches, width × depth × height). Specialty variants include taco clamshells (compartmented for taco bar service), tall sandwich clamshells, and high-dome variants for tall sandwiches. Case packs run 200–500 per case depending on size.

## PP and MFPP — when heat matters

Polypropylene (PP, resin code #5) is the right material when the food enters the container hot, when the customer will microwave the leftovers, or when the operation runs the same container through dishwasher cycles. PP is stable from -10°F to ~230°F. It survives microwave reheating, dishwasher cycles, and brief oven exposure (though not designed for prolonged oven use).

"Microwavable PP" (MFPP) is a marketing label that the FDA doesn't formally regulate — but in practice it refers to PP grades engineered for repeated microwave cycles without warping, leaching, or weakening. If your customers will reheat takeout in the microwave at home, MFPP is the right spec.

PP is recyclable (resin code #5) in theory but rarely accepted curbside for small-format items. The municipal sorting infrastructure built around #1 (PET) and #2 (HDPE) usually rejects #5 because the sorting machines can't separate it efficiently. This is a real environmental claim weakness for PP — it's recyclable on paper, not in practice.

## Fiber — the compostable option

Fiber clamshells (bagasse from sugarcane pulp, or molded paperboard) are the right answer in compostable-mandate markets — Washington State, California's SB 1383 catchment, parts of New York, Maryland, and an expanding list. They're BPI-certified compostable in industrial facilities (90 days, ~140°F sustained heat). They are NOT compostable in home compost bins, which is a buyer-education challenge.

Fiber clamshells without coating absorb moisture. Wet food (sauces, oily entrées) saturates the wall within 20–40 minutes. The mitigation is a PLA lining that creates a thin moisture barrier — but PLA-lined fiber costs ~2× plain bagasse. For dry food (sandwiches, baked goods) plain bagasse works fine. For wet food, budget for PLA-lined.

## EPS foam — declining fast

Expanded polystyrene foam (EPS, often called Styrofoam, though that's a trademark) was the standard cheap hot-food clamshell for 40 years. Its insulating properties are genuinely useful for hot food — a foam clamshell keeps food hot for ~30 minutes longer than a plastic equivalent. But:

- **Banned in 10+ US states and many cities** including Maryland, New Jersey, New York (statewide), Vermont, Maine, Washington, Oregon, Virginia, Delaware, Colorado, plus city-level bans in DC, San Francisco, Seattle, Portland, and dozens more
- **Not recyclable** in any meaningful sense — virtually no curbside program accepts it, and dedicated EPS recyclers are rare and require clean material
- **Customer perception** has shifted sharply against foam over the last decade

If you operate in a non-ban state and your customers don't care about environmental positioning, foam still has the lowest cost per unit. Outside of those narrow conditions, foam is the wrong long-term choice.

## Case-pack and pallet-density tradeoffs

Clamshell case packs are higher than most foodservice containers because they nest efficiently. Standard PET clamshell case packs run 200–500 per case. Pallet density is 40–80 cases per pallet depending on case dimensions. Two operational tradeoffs to consider:

- **Smaller case packs reduce dead inventory** when an operator only needs a few hundred per week. The unit cost goes up ~5–10% but the carrying cost goes down.
- **Larger case packs improve pallet utilization** for higher-volume operations. The break-even is typically 800–1000 units/week — above that, the larger case pack is worth the storage commitment.

## What about hinged vs separate-lid systems?

Hinged clamshells (one piece) are faster at the back-of-house. The lid is already attached, so packing a takeout order is a single motion. The downside: when the lid is damaged in transit, the whole container is unusable. With a separate-lid deli container, the operator can swap a fresh lid onto a good cup.

For high-volume operations with controlled transit (counter pickup, short delivery distances), hinged clamshells win on speed. For operations with longer transit windows or fragile food (eggs benedict, plated entrees), separate-lid systems offer more recovery options.

## Section 301 tariffs and clamshell sourcing

Plastic clamshells from China carry a 25% Section 301 tariff plus underlying duty rates. Aluminum and steel clamshell raw materials face additional duties. The effective landed cost difference between China-origin and Vietnam/Turkey/Mexico-origin clamshells has been 15–30% since 2024 — most US distributors have shifted sourcing accordingly, but you'll still see China-origin SKUs on the market at lower prices reflecting pre-tariff inventory.

## Summary cheat sheet

| Decision | Right answer |
| --- | --- |
| Cold food, visibility matters | **PET** clamshell |
| Hot food, customer reheats | **MFPP** clamshell |
| Compostable-mandate market | **Fiber** (PLA-lined if wet food) |
| Operating in foam-ban state | Anything but **EPS** |
| Eco-positioned brand in non-mandate market | **Fiber** for the brand signal |
| Lowest unit cost, no constraints | **EPS** (if legal in your state) |
